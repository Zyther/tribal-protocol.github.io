The Tribal Protocol: A Call to Action

It's all about Ownership

We believe the users — real people — should own and monetize the social networks they belong to and power. There needs to be a “people focus” on what web3 should be. We believe in designing first for non-technical everyday users, and hope to leave the focus on crypto and blockchain as non-blocking technical details.

Over the past 5 years there have been dozens of credible attempts at “decentralizing social”, but they have all been found wanting. At Tribal Protocol we are done waiting for someone else to solve this. We’re creating a layer 1 blockchain built for people to “Tribe” online, and we hope you’ll crack a laptop and join the fun.

What is Tribal Protocol?

“Tribes form so naturally it’s as though our tribe is part of our genetic code. Tribes helped humans survive the last ice age, build farming communities, and, later, cities. Birds flock, fish school, people ‘tribe’”

-Tribal Leadership

We are moving past the age of monolithic social networks, and moving towards a multitude of niche social networks where content and users flow freely.

The Tribal Protocol is a layer 1 blockchain designed for people to “tribe” online. Our primary mission is to allow anyone to become the co-founder of their own social networks, and in turn collectively change how people organize and socialize online.

Communities have been built online for over 20 years and the social feature innovation has been amazing to watch: feeds, groups, reels, lives, threads, clubhouses, likes, follows, and pokes. While many might think that the design space for social media is tapped out, we think that just around the corner is a place where all these innovative features can be remixed in a new category of social: niche, co-owned experiences tailored by and for specific communities and purposes.

We believe: When given the opportunity to own these communities, people will organize, manage, and scale social networks (”tribes”) of their own. What’s missing is the backend and no-code tools to make this a reality.

There are countless networks that should exist online today but don’t because of how difficult it is to bootstrap them. Tribal Protocol’s mission is to reduce the barrier to entry for these new social networks to form, and do so with ownership as an original system condition. Each tribe’s ability to have their own token incentives, participate in the economic upside, and governance of their network makes for a truly viable answer to overcoming the steep social media platform switching costs.

What are we doing that’s so fundamentally different it warrants it’s own full stack solution?

Key areas of innovation

  1. Each tribe is a DAO, meaning that:
  • They have a shared treasury and voting takes place to drive spending and system rules management.
  • They have their own token supply, a key component in gaining early adopters and co-owners.
  • They collectively determine their moderation norms.
  • As many tribal activities as possible are done autonomously via function specific tribal protocol smart contracts.
  1. Content is owned by users, meaning that:
  • Apps, Tribes, or any entity cannot view content without a lease. 100% of content is locked by default, and can only be “unlocked” by tribes that have leased the content from its owner. If anyone can paint a screen with your on chain content without your consent, that’s not ownership.
  • Content hosting cost is paid by the tribes leasing the content. This allows for decentralized content storage while also allowing users to “post for free” as they are accustomed to online today.
  • Users can flow through different tribes and online experiences and their content will remain in the places where it has the correct context - i.e. Linkedin content stays on Linkedin, IG content stays IG, and Medium content stays on Medium.
  1. No code social media platform creation and management - think Wix and Shopify for the creation and management of your own social media product. Imagine if a few friends with no coding experience could launch a social media platform for a community or cause that they were passionate about.

By owning the entire stack we can ensure that users will truly own their content, both users and tribes experience consumer friendly “gas” costs, and we retain the flexibility to implement a fully custom data structure optimized for storage heavy social media data at the protocol level itself.

Tribes (Social Apps, Organizations, Social Content Sites or Experiences) have native tools to overcome user switching costs and the “cold start” problem.

The cold start problem is solved for in two meaningful ways:

  1. User generated content: Every time a new users starts creating content anywhere on tribal protocol, that content can be “leased” by the user to any front end application or experience. You could imagine a product with content similar to Barstool Sports being ideated by a few college students - and on Day 1 of launching the site they could partner with content creators to “lease” relevant content in order to make their site engaging immediately.
  1. Switching cost: Token incentives are a very effective means for creating a passionate initial user base. Imagine that this same Barstool Sports like product airdropped 0.25% stake of their “seed round” tokens to 20 content creators that put out great relevant content to their site. They just gave away 5% of the co-owned network and effectively gained their first 20 users that also double as co-owners of the network.

Where we’ve been left wanting - A review of current Decentralized Social

*Incumbent social media has all kinds of issues we’re not super stoked about, but have been pretty well documented by every project in this space. Feel free to check out their white papers if you’re looking to scratch that itch.

There are no shortage of projects and protocols that have attempted to take on incumbent social media companies, with fairly modest progress thus far. With that said, we are still convinced that the “Decentralized Social” category will be the next major wave for web3.

Our overarching critique of these projects is this: They led with “decentralization” as a core user adoption strategy, while delivering web2 product replicas with web3 window dressing. There were no true user hooks; certainly not sufficient enough ones to address switching cost to get users in the door or the stickiness to keep them there.

A short list of projects by category:

Early attempts: Steemit.com, Hive.com, Minds.com

Attempts on ETH: Lens, Farcaster

Non-ETH based attempts: Solcial, SubSocial

Unique modern attempts: DeSo, DSNP

Each one of these products are compelling in their own ways - but all are still searching for novel ways to mix these web3 components in a way that will unlock network effects and overcome the steep switching costs inherent with joining new social networks.

What makes Tribal Protocol unique is our full stack approach to serve a very specific customer - people who want to become founders, co-owners, and contributors to entirely new niche social networks that they control. The vast majority of the space today is attempting to enable the “decentralized twitter or facebook” products while not actually enabling early stage social networks to form. Our most critical differentiator from all other attempts in this space is the creation of DAOs that are directly tied to niche social networks. The economic alignment that DAOs unlock, combined with the content moat from user driven content leasing, is the secret sauce that allow nascent social networks to form.

Tech Principles

There are a few technical principles we’re centered around:

  1. Decentralization: While this doesn’t really matter to users in the early days, it does matter for actual ownership if this protocol is successful and scales. By building with a substrate foundation we leave ourselves open to launching to mainnet as either a stand alone L1 or as a Polkadot parachain, depending on technical discovery during the build out.
  1. Open Source: Our developer adoption program will focus on building a cadre of open source contributors to the full stack through bounties, hackathons, genuine engagement, as well as building out a full time core development team, primarily based out of Miami.
    1. Core protocol open source
    1. Front end SDKs, Orchestrator, Content Moderation Tooling, Component libraries
  1. Ownership, Content: The value of a social network is directly related to its users and their content. User ownership of content is just one of the promises of web3 that we seek to fulfill. With the Tribal Protocol:
    1. Creators always own their content.
    1. The creator pays the one time content storage fee.
    1. The creator chooses to lease the content to any tribe(s) that agree to host it. A content lease dictates which tribes are able to access and display content.
    1. The hosting tribe pays a lease fee back to the creator that covers the one-time storage fee.
    1. Either party (creator or tribe) can end a lease at any time for any reason giving the user complete control of where their content is seen and used.
    1. The creator can delete their content from the Tribal chain (without deleting the actual transactions), ensuring the right to be forgotten.
  1. Ownership, Networks: Another key promise of web3 is that the same users that power social networks can be owners of the platforms they power. The Tribal Protocol provides a collection of utilities, and modules that assist with:
    1. Governance
    1. L2 Tokenization/Treasury
    1. Communication/Coordination
    1. Other functionality to create, and maintain Decentralized Autonomous Organizations.

Tech Stack

The Tribal Protocol is a layer 1 blockchain, supported by Substrate.

Substrate provides libraries, and tooling written in rust-lang that will be used as the scaffolding of our entire blockchain. Substrate as a platform provides many powerful blockchain features, such as smart contract capabilities (ink!), block production, genesis, consensus, and finalization.

Tribal Protocol’s smart contracts are written in ink!.

The CLI, and other core components use reputable CLI libraries, and tokio to provide asynchronous task execution from within our libraries.

The majority of Tribal’s codebase is rust, including many client/browser-side utilities to help users with the complexity of transaction handling. These client-side utilities are wrapped in a module we are calling the Orchestrator.

The Orchestrator is be a client-side WASM library, not server-side. This is where interaction between smart contracts on chain and UI happen. Additionally this allows private keys to remain client side, invisible to the chain.

All cryptographic functions will be derived from stock substrate libraries, and pallets.

Most lower-level blockchain related functionality will initially use stock substrate pallets like BABE and/or GRANDPA. Upon further technical discovery the team will pursue either a mainnet launch as a Polkadot Parachain or as a stand alone L1.

Content Storage is a critical component of a social network. We are considering a hybrid proof-of-storage, and DHT key/value storage based content solution to roll as a native content solution. We expect to support industry-standard content storage solutions to be interoperable with the Tribal Protocol. Some of these solutions may include: IPFS, Ceramic, and Arweave.

Each tribe will be governed via their set of relevant tribal protocol smart contracts. Key system level contracts include:

  • Create Tribe
  • Create Content
  • Lease Content / Revoke Lease
  • Tribe Governance (Treasury, Tribe Rules, User Actions)

Tech Goodies

Example: Create Tribe — Initiate “Create Tribe” request

To create a tribe, one founder submits a create_tribe contract transaction, proxied via the Orchestrator. The Orchestrator submits the signed transaction to the smart contract itself, digests the response, and returns the specified tribe’s public_key to the founder.

Once the above operation is complete, tribes are left in a disabled state, until all listed founders approve the creation of the tribe.

Lease Content

Content leasing is how we expect to enable individual content ownership and distribution. Leasing expects 2 actors: the content creator (lessor) and the tribe (lessee).

The lease_content contract creates the on-chain representation between the specified tribe and the user-owned content by creating a cryptographic key that unlocks the original cryptographic content key which is used to decrypt that specific user’s content.

This model allows content to be encrypted with a single key-pair, that is stored using the user’s own key-pair. A lease will decrypt the original content key, and create a new cryptographic key which can also unlock the original content’s key. Any key that is able to unlock the original content key will be derived from the original.

Our guiding principles:

Buzz words are for weasels

We could use all the buzz words to describe this protocol, but we won’t. We’ll use words that mean things and speak to user adoption and retention drivers. Users don’t care about your back-end, which is why Twitter doesn’t expect their microservice architecture to drive user adoption.

Our focus is on innovating and building better infrastructure for the digital economy. We believe that a custom layer 1 blockchain is the best tool for that in our case.

“The Back-end” only matters at scale

Creating a blockchain based social protocol is much, much harder than creating a centralized one. We’ll actually need to work 10x harder to make delighting user experiences on a blockchain back-end, but getting users to onboard into crypto without even knowing they are is the key to creating the future we all want to live in. We want users to be delighted by decentralization, not beaten over the head with it.

WII FM

The biggest downfall of products, and “decentralized social” products especially, is that they fail to recognize that every user is busy listening to a radio station called “What’s in it for me”. Every single product decision of tribal protocol optimizes for providing obvious value to a user, with the lowest possible friction to get to that ah-hah moment.

The switching cost from one given social network to a new one is enormous. If we expect entrepreneurs and web2 social users to build and join new experiences online, the pastures need to be significantly greener than their current ones.
